Instead of using friction to transmit power, gears use its teeth to create contact patches which they can apply leverage to. With teeth you cannot have a completely tangent force vector as you do with a friction drive.

The only point of zero sliding (no slipping) is a the pitch diameter. The rest of the contact between the teeth is sliding. Before off loading one tooth, a second tooth is engaged to share the load to prevent vibrations. The path of contact must be a straight line which is tangent to both pitch diameters. 
>[!question]
>Why does it have to travel on a straight line?

>[!answer]
> It ensures that the forces act along the same line of action

### Common Gears
- Spur - planar motion, easy to manufacture
- [[Helical Gears]] - planar motion, quieter operation, withstand more load.
- Crossed helical - can change axis of motion or remain in the same plane. Directional, can only mesh in one direction.
- [[Bevel Gears]] - changes plane of motion. 
- Worm - A screw that permits massive gear ratios since each revolution only advances a single tooth. Changes axis of motion. No common line of action, so there is no point of pure rolling, only slipping. 
- Internal
- Rack and pinion - converts rotational to linear motion. 
- Hypoid gears - a bevel gear that does not act normal to the tangent of the pitch diameter.

#### Gear Ratio
Often the pinion (the driven gear (input)) is smaller than the driven gear, so there is a speed reduction. It is commonly written as 1:n where the ratio is how much slower the output is compared to the input. If the output is faster, it must be labelled as an overdrive.

### Involute Geometry

We are trying to balance the optimization of reducing radial force while keeping strength in the teeth. This is primarily determined by the pressure angle of the teeth.

Increasing pressure angle increases radial forces, but allows teeth to be thicker.

##### Pitch
 In the imperial world diametrical pitch is used 
 $$
P=\frac{N}{d}
$$
In metric the pitch is referenced to as the distance per tooth along the pitch circumference.
$$
p=\frac{\pi d}{N}
$$
##### Modulus
Modulus is a very important metric it is just a ratio.
$$
m=\frac{d}{N}
$$
#### Contact Ratio
The contact ratio tells you how often two teeth are in contact compared to how often one tooth is in contact.

This contact ratio is dependent on the addendum length. 

If the addendum becomes too long, the gears will jam which is called interference. This is when contact starts before the tangent line that connects the duodenum circles.

To prevent interference, keep the number of teeth on the pinion gear above a minimum.
