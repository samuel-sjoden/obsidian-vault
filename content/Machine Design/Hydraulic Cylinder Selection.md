#### Cushions
Cushions are put in place inside of the piston to prevent the piston from slamming into the end of the case. This is done with a spear on the end of the piston which blocks the outflow before the piston head reaches the end of the body. This dampens the motion of the piston. Since the fluid is still incompressible, having the piston slam into the fluid still produces shock.

![[Pasted image 20251021113214.png]]
To release the pressure built up, there is a small release channel that allows for a much slower fluid escape.
#### Cylinder Stroke Determination
Piston cylinders are effectivley columns and expericence [[Column Stress]], so their most likley failure mode is buckling.

The critical failure for buckling is dependent on the length of the pillar and the type of mounting that the cylinder has. The more freedom the column has to move in directions other than the direction of the load, the sooner the column will fail.


![[Pasted image 20251021114823.png]]
This table describes different cylinder mounting and their "stroke factor". The stroke factor is a value in the denominator so smaller is better. 

>[!Justification]
> We use pivot mounts on things like moving booms where the direction that the force is applied is different.

##### Shaft Geometry
Another factor that contributes to the buckling failure mode is the diameter of the of the shaft. Larger diameter shafts resist buckling better.

##### Stop Tubes
Stop tubes are an additional component but in place to prevent bending on the shaft. They support the piston internally to distribute the load generated from the torque applied to the cylinder.

### Piston Speed
Piston speed must be selected appropriatley from the flow rate availible while keeping fluid velocity under $15\frac{\text{m}}{s}$ to reduce [[Hydraulic Shock]], [[Turbulence]], and pressure loss.
